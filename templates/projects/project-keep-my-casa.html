Keep My Casa: ATX Hack for Change Project to Explore Community Problem and Identify a Software Solution

Back
Keep My Casa is a mobile-first, web application developed to help Austin, Texas residents with rising property taxes keep their homes.

PROJECT DETAILS
Need: Property taxes are rising at a rapid rate in Austin. Many homeowners losing their properties due to this spike are low-income, low-literacy, or retired. There are resources available to help these individuals, but this information is not readily accessible or is hard to navigate to. Many of these users access their information through mobile devices.
Objective: Design a mobile-first, web application prototype that will conversationally guide an individual at risk of losing their property to the information that they need based on their background.
Approach: We identified how to design for the target market, typically low-literacy users. We studied usability techniques and how to translate them into our final functional prototype.
Project Duration: 2 weeks; Summer 2017; To Be Continued in 2018
Group Members: Joanne Bruno, Jeremy Cantu, Kaitlyn Clark, Jim Given, Jess Sauer
My Role(s): UI/UX Designer, Front-End Developer
Final Result: We delivered a mobile-first, web application functional prototype that is WCAG 2.0/Section 508 compliant (web accessibility guidelines). Project won "Best Design" award at hackathon. Project afterwards was presented in front of city leaders at Austin Open Civic Meetup.

UX Process

I. Plan, Identify, Understand

Austin, Texas in recent years has had a sharp increase in property taxes, sometimes at 25 to 30 percent. For some homeowners, especially those who are low-income, elderly, or retired, this increase is forcing them to sell or leave their properties. At the time of the project, there are many resources available online for property owners at risk for losing their homes. However, this information is scattered across various sites, not readily accessible, and can be cumbersome for the homeowner to narrow down to their specific needs. We created the Keep My Casa web application to help address this civic need.

II. Iterative Design

After compiling list of resources into an excel document, and identifying the target audience, our team collectively whiteboarded a user flow of question prompts to direct the homeowner to customized results based on their responses. Through our market research, we identified that typically low-income or low-literate users prefer a "chat" interface and have higher access to a mobile interface versus a desktop. Therefore we geared our flow to be more conversational and simplified.

Before beginning the development phase, a mock form was created based on the designed flow. We consulted with industry experts in this area to better adjust the language used and iterate on simplification of the UI. The biggest challenge was reducing the complexity of the question selection processes, as this task can be difficult for novice tech users.

After completing the mockups, I designed our logo going forward to symbolize the support and strength we wish to provide through this service, at their time of need. At this point we chose our name to be the bilingual title: "Keep My Casa", since through our research we discovered that the majority of those at risk of losing their homes in Austin, Texas are hispanic. In future work, I hope to fully translate our application to Spanish to better accommodate those users.

III. Development

The wireframes were translated into a fully functional, mobile-first, web application the aligned with our user flow mockup. The UI was developed using AngularJS, AngularJs Material, HTML, and CSS. This technology stack enabled the developers to quickly prototype the app while keeping design consistency in mind.

IV. Evaluate

Through Keep My Casa, I learned the value of keeping accessibility and design in mind for low-literate, tech novice, or elderly users. I now apply web accessibility guideline principles (WCAG) through my more recent projects, as they enable the general population to have a greater experience with your app.

This project was awarded best design at the hackathon, for its use of accessibility principles with the end user in mind and its overall visual aesthetic.

This project was later presented in front of city representatives at a Open Austin Civic meetup. For future work, we hope to solidify a minimum viable product that can be released to the public.
