Medidata: Electronic Health Record (EHR) Functional Prototype

Back
Medidata is an Electronic Health Record (EHR) functional prototype designed strategically to simplify patient medical data, thereby, allowing doctors to more quickly review patient history prior to visits.

PROJECT DETAILS
Need: Hospitals are rapidly adopting Electronic Health Records (EHR) systems. Currently, these systems have an overwhelmingly amount of data stored per patient. Via market research, it was discovered that doctors are spending on average 10-15 minutes reading this data before meeting with patients.
Objective: Develop an interface that increases doctor's productivity using the EHR, thereby reducing patient wait times.
Approach: Designed a web application with four main modules (generic patient information header, ICD9 wheel, timeline of patient’s previous visits, and clinical note session) that utilize a RESTful API to pull data from a mock patient database. The information was structured using: search-centric design, sequential ordering, expandable views, color labeling, and graphical views.
Project Duration: 6 months; Spring 2015
Group Members: Joanne Bruno, Derek Burgman, Matthew Snyder, Michael Draughon
My Role(s): UI UX Lead, Front-End Developer, Project Manager, Scribe
Final Result: Created an interactive EHR web application as a functional prototype to better visualize patient data through the use of a relevancy filterable timeline and by mapping patient history over an ICD9 Tree.

UX Process

I. Plan, Identify, Understand

Could you easily understand the following EHR at-a-glance?

With the increase in the number of Electronic Health Record (EHR) systems being installed in modern hospitals, the demand for complex EHR data software solutions has elevated. Although information is now digitized, doctors are still spending additional time sifting through incredulous amounts of information with EHR user interfaces. Sponsors from the VA, Scott And White Hospital, and Texas A&M biomedical engineering have consulted our group in order to develop a software prototype component to reduce the amount of time doctors spend finding relevant details before they begin diagnosis of a patient.

Application Diagram

To deliver a product that would satisfy our client's needs, we identified the following top items to focus on based on our studies and suggestions from client interviews. Our clients heavily emphasized elements involving the user interface, the usability of the application, and the functionality specifications of various components. The user interface needs to contain concise textual information, a method of searching for patients, and be responsive to the various devices with browser capability. This allows for the physician to view the information in a manner that is efficiently organized and allows for a smoother user experience. We identified the engineering requirements needed to fulfill this need.

Furthermore, the electronic health record needs to be usable in terms of having a minimal learning curve for the doctors to easily access all information without extensive training and also be accessible from a web browser that is familiar to the majority of users. Functionality should include relevant filters on the textual patient information such as the timeline, data information and retrieval. The application should interface with multiple databases.

II. Iterative Design

The timeline will display only the most pertinent patient visit information for each date listed in the database. Based on the compiled suggestions of interviewed medical physicians, the information displayed in the timeline shall include the ICD9 code diagnosis, type of visit, and chief complaint. In the upper right hand corner, the timeline can be filtered by several options. For example, a physician may choose to view only the patient visits within the past year. Upon selecting a specific date in the timeline, the other modules will update to comply with that specific date. This will allow for the most effective use of space, as the clinical notes for the visit will be relevant and the ICD9 diagnosis of the particular date will be highlighted in the lower left of the screen. The implementation of all the timeline will be accomplished using Cascading Style Sheets (CSS), as the complexity of this module is relatively low.

The ICD9 tree serves to effectively visualize the nodes visited, in other words, the diagnoses given to the patient in the form of ICD9 codes. Our implementation was in the form of a ring, created using the data-driven documents (D3.js) JavaScript library. This library will help produce an effectively interactive and dynamic visualization of the ICD9 data. Thus, we will be able to achieve improved module performance for standard web browsers to enhance the user experience of the physician. Furthermore, the tree will update to highlight the most relevant nodes based on the dates clicked in the timeline module. For example, if the user would like to see only the ICD9 codes diagnosed for the more recent patient visit on the timeline, he or she may simply can click on the date in the timeline to view the updated nodes on the tree for that visit.

After analyzing various user interfaces of electronic health records and the compilation of surveyed information from our clients, the following user interface design was proposed by me. In this minimalistic design there is one homepage with expandable views. The user can click on the upper right hand corner of each widget in order to maximize the viewing space and see more information. Furthermore, the timeline has an additional button in the upper right hand corner in order to access filtering functions. When a timeline date is clicked, all relevant information on the screen will automatically in the upper header of the screen, the most pertinent patient information is displayed. Physicians can search patient in the text field in the upper left hand corner under the logo of the application. Patients can be searched by various fields, such as name, patient identification number, and social security. Each widget is clearly identifiable and strives to indicate their affordances. Hierarchical navigation is eliminated in order to reduce the amount of time a physician may spend browsing through the interface to acquire the information he or she may need. Thus, the design efficiently spans to the size of the screen.

III. Development

For our website, we have designed four main modules that utilize a RESTful API to pull data from a mock patient database. Our four modules are a header including generic patient information, a timeline of patient’s previous visits, an ICD9 wheel highlighting the ICD9 codes that occurred in the patient’s history, and a clinical note section detailing the specifics of a chosen visit in the patient’s history. These four modules are all shown on a single page in the website with the ability to expand modules for better viewing when desired. A key feature that our website provides is the ability to filter the patient’s history based on their current chief complaint, a certain time frame, or for a specific subset of ICD9 codes.

The framework implementation is built off of a template called “Clip Two” constructed with Bootstrap and AngularJS. Using AngularJS allows us to simplify module implementation and gives us dependency injection. We have modified and added code to the template to best suit our needs in terms of constructing the individual modules.

We implemented a mock patient database server using WampServer, MySQL, and a RESTFul php interface that will be used to serve our anonymized patient information to be displayed on the website.

IV. Evaluate

We have created a complete functional prototype, web application, that has been approved by the biomedical team as well as having passed all the acceptance and integration tests that we formed during the design of the system. This process involved learning how to efficiently collaborate with a hierarchy of clients and the complexities that arise when working with government technologies. The most immediate goal for the future of the project is to attach it to a real patient database rather than the mock patient database that we have been using. We would also like to have included cross-platform and cross-browser support for the website. From a user survey conducted with 15 medical professionals, results indicated that our application was exceptionally user friendly and showed preference for the use or addition of our work into their current EHR system.
